class="small text-muted" href="/account/join/" rel="nofollow"> <i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-info-circle"> </i> Remove ads</a>\n</div>\n<p>Big data. Cloud data. AI training data and personally identifying data. Data is all around you and is growing every day. It only makes sense that software engineering has evolved to include <strong>data engineering</strong>, a subdiscipline that focuses directly on the transportation, transformation, and storage of data.</p>\n<p>Perhaps you\xe2\x80\x99ve seen big data job postings and are intrigued by the prospect of handling petabyte-scale data. Maybe you\xe2\x80\x99re curious about how <a href="https://realpython.com/generative-adversarial-networks/">generative adversarial networks</a> create realistic images from underlying data. Maybe you\xe2\x80\x99ve never even heard of data engineering but are interested in how developers handle the vast amounts of data necessary for most applications today.</p>\n<p>No matter which category you fall into, this introductory article is for you. You\xe2\x80\x99ll get a broad overview of the field, including what data engineering is and what kind of work it entails.</p>\n<p><strong>In this article, you\xe2\x80\x99ll learn:</strong></p>\n<ul>\n<li>What the <strong>current state</strong> of the data engineering field is</li>\n<li>How data engineering is <strong>used in the industry</strong></li>\n<li>Who the various <strong>customers</strong> of data engineers are</li>\n<li>What is and what isn\xe2\x80\x99t part of the <strong>data engineering field</strong></li>\n<li>How to decide if you want to <strong>pursue data engineering</strong> as a discipline</li>\n</ul>\n<p>To begin, you\xe2\x80\x99ll answer one of the most pressing questions about the field: What do data engineers do, anyway?</p>\n<div class="alert alert-warning" role="alert"><p><strong>Free Bonus:</strong> <a class="alert-link" data-focus="false" data-target="#modal-python-cheat-sheet-shortened" data-toggle="modal" href="">Click here to get a Python Cheat Sheet</a> and learn the basics of Python 3, like working with data types, dictionaries, lists, and Python functions.</p></div>\n<section class="section2" id="what-do-data-engineers-do"><h2>What Do Data Engineers Do?<a class="headerlink" href="#what-do-data-engineers-do" title="Permanent link"></a></h2>\n<p>Data engineering is a very broad discipline that comes with multiple titles. In many organizations, it may not even have a specific title. Because of this, it\xe2\x80\x99s probably best to first identify the goals of data engineering and then discuss what kind of work brings about the desired outcomes.</p>\n<p>The ultimate goal of data engineering is to provide organized, consistent data flow to enable data-driven work, such as:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Training machine learning models</li>\n<li>Doing exploratory data analysis</li>\n<li>Populating fields in an application with outside data</li>\n</ul>\n<p>This data flow can be achieved in any number of ways, and the specific tool sets, techniques, and skills required will vary widely across teams, organizations, and desired outcomes. However, a common pattern is the <strong>data pipeline</strong>. This is a system that consists of independent programs that do various operations on incoming or collected data. </p>\n<p>Data pipelines are often distributed across multiple servers:</p>\n<figure class="js-lightbox"><a href="https://files.realpython.com/media/example_pipeline_editable.1f5c28b7013f.png" target="_blank"><img alt="A simplified diagram of a data pipeline showing multiple sources being extracted into a data lake, transformed across multiple independent segments, then stored in a production database" class="img-fluid mx-auto d-block" data-asset="3102" height="168" loading="lazy" sizes="75vw" src="https://files.realpython.com/media/example_pipeline_editable.1f5c28b7013f.png" srcset="https://robocrop.realpython.net/?url=https%3A//files.realpython.com/media/example_pipeline_editable.1f5c28b7013f.png&amp;w=192&amp;sig=9bd4f0d3a7f3a32b5439d73d7ee0deec4a2c6c78 192w, https://robocrop.realpython.net/?url=https%3A//files.realpython.com/media/example_pipeline_editable.1f5c28b7013f.png&amp;w=385&amp;sig=19906d839ad6b753815fd6817219e962ab2ae5c7 385w, https://files.realpython.com/media/example_pipeline_editable.1f5c28b7013f.png 771w" width="771"/></a></figure>\n<p>This image is a simplified example data pipeline to give you a very basic idea of an architecture you may encounter. You\xe2\x80\x99ll see a more complex representation further down.</p>\n<p>The data can come from any source:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="https://realpython.com/micropython/">Internet of Things</a> devices</li>\n<li>Vehicle telemetry</li>\n<li>Real estate data feeds</li>\n<li>Normal user activity on a web application</li>\n<li>Any other collection or measurement tools you can think of </li>\n</ul>\n<p>Depending on the nature of these sources, the incoming data will be processed in real-time <strong>streams</strong> or at some regular cadence in <strong>batches</strong>.</p>\n<p>The pipeline that the data runs through is the responsibility of the data engineer. Data engineering teams are responsible for the design, construction, maintenance, extension, and often, the infrastructure that supports data pipelines. They may also be responsible for the incoming data or, more often, the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_model">data model</a> and how that data is finally stored. </p>\n<p>If you think about the data pipeline as a type of application, then data engineering starts to look like any other software engineering discipline.</p>\n<p>Many teams are also moving toward building <strong>data platforms</strong>. In many organizations, it\xe2\x80\x99s not enough to have just a single pipeline saving incoming data to an SQL database somewhere. Large organizations have multiple teams that need different levels of access to different kinds of data.</p>\n<p>For example, <strong>artificial intelligence (AI)</strong> teams may need ways to label and split cleaned data. <strong>Business intelligence (BI)</strong> teams may need easy access to aggregate data and build data visualizations. <strong>Data science</strong> teams may need database-level access to properly explore the data. </p>\n<p>If you\xe2\x80\x99re familiar with web development, then you might find this structure similar to the <a href="https://realpython.com/the-model-view-controller-mvc-paradigm-summarized-with-legos/">Model-View-Controller (MVC) design pattern</a>. With MVC, data engineers are responsible for the model, AI or BI teams work on the views, and all groups collaborate on the controller. Building data platforms that serve all these needs is becoming a major priority in organizations with diverse teams that rely on data access. </p>\n<p>Now that you\xe2\x80\x99ve seen some of what data engineers do and how intertwined they are with the customers they serve, it\xe2\x80\x99ll be helpful to learn a bit more about those customers and what responsibilities data engineers have to them.</p>\n<div><div class="rounded border border-light" style="display:block;position:relative;"><div style="display:block;width:100%;padding-top:12.5%;"></div><div class="rpad rounded border" data-unit="8x1" style="position:absolute;left:0;top:0;right:0;bottom:0;overflow:hidden;"></div></div><a class="small text-muted" href="/account/join/" rel="nofollow"><i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-info-circle"> </i> Remove ads</a></div></section><section class="section2" id="what-are-the-responsibilities-of-data-engineers"><h2>What Are the Responsibilities of Data Engineers?<a class="headerlink" href="#what-are-the-responsibilities-of-data-engineers" title="Permanent link"></a></h2>\n<p>The customers that rely on data engineers are as diverse as the skills and outputs of the data engineering teams themselves. No matter what field you pursue, your customers will always determine what problems you solve and how you solve them.</p>\n<p>In this section, you\xe2\x80\x99ll learn about a few common customers of data engineering teams through the lens of their data needs:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Data science and AI teams</li>\n<li>Business intelligence or analytics teams</li>\n<li>Product teams</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Before any of these teams can work effectively, certain needs have to be met. In particular, the data must be:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Reliably routed into the wider system</li>\n<li>Normalized to a sensible data model</li>\n<li>Cleaned to fill in important gaps</li>\n<li>Made accessible to all relevant to members</li>\n</ul>\n<p>These requirements are more fully detailed in the excellent article <a href="https://hackernoon.com/the-ai-hierarchy-of-needs-18f111fcc007">The AI Hierarchy of Needs</a> by Monica Rogarty. As a data engineer, you\xe2\x80\x99re responsible for addressing your customers\xe2\x80\x99 data needs. However, you\xe2\x80\x99ll use a variety of approaches to accommodate their individual workflows. </p>\n<section class="section3" id="data-flow"><h3>Data Flow<a class="headerlink" href="#data-flow" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>To do anything with data in a system, you must first ensure that it can flow into and through the system reliably. Inputs can be almost any type of data you can imagine, including:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Live streams of <a href="https://realpython.com/python-json/">JSON</a> or XML data</li>\n<li>Batches of videos updated every hour</li>\n<li>Monthly blood-draw data</li>\n<li>Weekly batches of labeled images</li>\n<li>Telemetry from deployed sensors</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Data engineers are often responsible for consuming this data, designing a system that can take this data as input from one or many sources, transform it, and then store it for their customers. These systems are often called <strong>ETL</strong> pipelines, which stands for <strong>extract</strong>, <strong>transform</strong>, and <strong>load</strong>.</p>\n<p>The data flow responsibility mostly falls under the <strong>extract</strong> step. But the data engineer\xe2\x80\x99s responsibility doesn\xe2\x80\x99t stop at pulling data into the pipeline. They have to ensure that the pipeline is robust enough to stay up in the face of unexpected or malformed data, sources going offline, and fatal bugs. Uptime is very important, especially when you\xe2\x80\x99re consuming live or time-sensitive data.</p>\n<p>Your responsibility to maintain data flow will be pretty consistent no matter who your customer is. However, some customers can be more demanding than others, especially when the customer is an application that relies on data being updated in real time.</p>\n</section><section class="section3" id="data-normalization-and-modeling"><h3>Data Normalization and Modeling<a class="headerlink" href="#data-normalization-and-modeling" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>Data flowing into a system is great. However, at some point, the data need to conform to some kind of architectural standard. <strong>Normalizing</strong> data involves tasks that make the data more accessible to users. This includes but is not limited to the following steps:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Removing duplicates (<strong>deduplication</strong>)</li>\n<li>Fixing conflicting data</li>\n<li>Conforming data to a specified data model</li>\n</ul>\n<p>These processes may happen at different stages. For example, imagine you work in a large organization with data scientists and a BI team, both of whom rely on your data. You may store unstructured data in a <strong>data lake</strong> to be used by your data science customers for exploratory data analysis. You may also store the normalized data in a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relational_database">relational database</a> or a more purpose-built <strong>data warehouse</strong> to be used by the BI team in its reports.</p>\n<p>You may have more or fewer customer teams or perhaps an application that consumes your data. The image below shows a modified version of the previous pipeline example, highlighting the different stages at which certain teams may access the data:</p>\n<figure class="js-lightbox"><a href="https://files.realpython.com/media/de_illus_editable.e091cb69d248.png" target="_blank"><img alt="An example pipeline including customer teams at various stages. Data scientists at the data lake, business intelligence at the more organized data warehouse, and the app at the production database" class="img-fluid mx-auto d-block" data-asset="3103" height="271" loading="lazy" sizes="75vw" src="https://files.realpython.com/media/de_illus_editable.e091cb69d248.png" srcset="https://robocrop.realpython.net/?url=https%3A//files.realpython.com/media/de_illus_editable.e091cb69d248.png&amp;w=227&amp;sig=0c7777b8818aec60006a3603b8bd89bafa45b59e 227w, https://robocrop.realpython.net/?url=https%3A//files.realpython.com/media/de_illus_editable.e091cb69d248.png&amp;w=455&amp;sig=99ad3405220955d5b5f38ea1da935b086bc23203 455w, https://files.realpython.com/media/de_illus_editable.e091cb69d248.png 911w" width="911"/></a></figure>\n<p>In this image, you see a hypothetical data pipeline and the stages at which you\xe2\x80\x99ll often find different customer teams working.</p>\n<p>If your customer is a product team, then a well-architected data model is crucial. A thoughtful data model can be the difference between a slow, barely responsive application and one that runs as if it already knows what data the user wants to access. These sorts of decisions are often the result of a collaboration between product and data engineering teams.</p>\n<p>Data normalization and modeling are usually part of the <strong>transform</strong> step of ETL, but they\xe2\x80\x99re not the only ones in this category. Another common transformative step is data cleaning.</p>\n<div><div class="rounded border border-light" style="display:block;position:relative;"><div style="display:block;width:100%;padding-top:12.5%;"></div><div class="rpad rounded border" data-unit="8x1" style="position:absolute;left:0;top:0;right:0;bottom:0;overflow:hidden;"></div></div><a class="small text-muted" href="/account/join/" rel="nofollow"><i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-info-circle"> </i> Remove ads</a></div></section><section class="section3" id="data-cleaning"><h3>Data Cleaning<a class="headerlink" href="#data-cleaning" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p><strong>Data cleaning</strong> goes hand-in-hand with data normalization. Some even consider data normalization to be a subset of data cleaning. But while data normalization is mostly focused on making disparate data conform to some data model, data cleaning includes a number of actions that make the data more uniform and complete, including:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Casting the same data to a single type (for example, forcing <a href="https://realpython.com/convert-python-string-to-int/">strings in an integer field to be integers</a>)</li>\n<li>Ensuring dates are in the same format</li>\n<li>Filling in missing fields if possible</li>\n<li>Constraining values of a field to a specified range</li>\n<li>Removing corrupt or unusable data</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Data cleaning can fit into the deduplication and unifying data model steps in the diagram above. In reality, though, each of those steps is very large and can comprise any number of stages and individual processes.</p>\n<p>The specific actions you take to clean the data will be highly dependent on the inputs, data model, and desired outcomes. The importance of clean data, though, is constant:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Data scientists</strong> need it to perform accurate analyses. </li>\n<li><strong>Machine learning engineers</strong> need it to build accurate and generalizable models.</li>\n<li><strong>Business intelligence teams</strong> need it to provide accurate reports and forecasts to the business.</li>\n<li><strong>Product teams</strong> need it to ensure their product doesn\xe2\x80\x99t crash or give faulty information to users.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>The data-cleaning responsibility falls on many different shoulders and is dependent on the overall organization and its priorities. As a data engineer, you should strive to automate cleaning as much as possible and do regular spot checks on incoming and stored data. Your customer teams and leadership can provide insight on what constitutes clean data for their purposes.</p>\n</section><section class="section3" id="data-accessibility"><h3>Data Accessibility<a class="headerlink" href="#data-accessibility" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>Data accessibility doesn\xe2\x80\x99t get as much attention as data normalization and cleaning, but it\xe2\x80\x99s arguably one of the more important responsibilities of a customer-centric data engineering team.</p>\n<p><strong>Data accessibility</strong> refers to how easy the data is for customers to access and understand. This is something that is defined very differently depending on the customer:</p>\n<ul>\n<li><strong>Data science teams</strong> may simply need data that\xe2\x80\x99s accessible with some kind of query language. </li>\n<li><strong>Analytics teams</strong> may prefer data grouped by some metric, accessible through either basic queries or a reporting interface.</li>\n<li><strong>Product teams</strong> will often want data that is accessible through fast and straightforward queries that don\xe2\x80\x99t change often, with an eye toward product performance and reliability.</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Because larger organizations provide these teams and others with the same data, many have moved towards developing their own internal platforms for their disparate teams. A great mature example of this is the ride-hailing service Uber, which has shared many of the details of its <a href="https://eng.uber.com/uber-big-data-platform/">impressive big data platform</a>.</p>\n<p>In fact, many data engineers are finding themselves becoming platform engineers, making clear the continued importance of data engineering skills to data-driven businesses. Because data accessibility is intimately tied to how data is stored, it\xe2\x80\x99s a major component of the <strong>load</strong> step of ETL, which refers to how data is stored for later use.</p>\n<p>Now that you\xe2\x80\x99ve met some common data engineering customers and learned about their needs, it\xe2\x80\x99s time to look more closely at what skills you can develop to help address those needs.</p>\n</section></section><section class="section2" id="what-are-common-data-engineering-skills"><h2>What Are Common Data Engineering Skills?<a class="headerlink" href="#what-are-common-data-engineering-skills" title="Permanent link"></a></h2>\n<p>Data engineering skills are largely the same ones you need for software engineering. However, there are a few areas on which data engineers tend to have a greater focus. In this section, you\xe2\x80\x99ll learn about several important skill sets:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>General programming concepts</li>\n<li>Databases</li>\n<li>Distributed systems and cloud engineering</li>\n</ul>\n<p>Each of these will play a crucial role in making you a well-rounded data engineer.</p>\n<section class="section3" id="general-programming-skills"><h3>General Programming Skills<a class="headerlink" href="#general-programming-skills" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>Data engineering is a specialization of software engineering, so it makes sense that the fundamentals of software engineering are at the top of this list. As with other software engineering specializations, data engineers should understand design concepts such as <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don%27t_repeat_yourself">DRY (don\xe2\x80\x99t repeat yourself)</a>, <a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/object-oriented-programming-oop-python/">object-oriented programming</a>, <a href="https://realpython.com/python-data-structures/">data structures</a>, and algorithms.</p>\n<p>As in other specialties, there are also a few favored languages. As of this writing, the ones you see most often in data engineering job descriptions are Python, Scala, and <a href="https://realpython.com/oop-in-python-vs-java/">Java</a>. What makes these languages so popular?</p>\n<p>Python is popular for several reasons. One of the biggest is its ubiquity. By many measures, Python is among the top three most popular programming languages in the world. For example, it ranked second in the November 2020 <a href="https://www.tiobe.com/tiobe-index/">TIOBE Community Index</a> and third in Stack Overflow\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="https://stackoverflow.blog/2020/05/27/2020-stack-overflow-developer-survey-results/">2020 Developer Survey</a>.</p>\n<p>It\xe2\x80\x99s also widely used by machine learning and AI teams. Teams that work closely together often need to be able to communicate in the same language, and Python is still the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca">lingua franca</a> of the field.</p>\n<p>Another, more targeted reason for Python\xe2\x80\x99s popularity is its use in orchestration tools like <a href="https://airflow.apache.org">Apache Airflow</a> and the available libraries for popular tools like <a href="https://airflow.apache.org">Apache Spark</a>. If an organization uses tools like these, then it\xe2\x80\x99s essential to know the languages they make use of.</p>\n<p><a href="https://www.scala-lang.org/">Scala</a> is also quite popular, and like Python, this is partially due to the popularity of tools that use it, especially Apache Spark. Scala is a functional language that runs on the Java Virtual Machine (JVM), making it able to be used seamlessly with Java.</p>\n<p><a href="https://www.java.com/en/">Java</a> isn\xe2\x80\x99t quite as popular in data engineering, but you\xe2\x80\x99ll still see it in quite a few job descriptions. This is partially because of its ubiquity in enterprise software stacks and partially because of its interoperability with Scala. With Scala being used for Apache Spark, it makes sense that some teams make use of Java as well.</p>\n<p>In addition to general programming skills, a good familiarity with database technologies is essential.</p>\n<div><div class="rounded border border-light" style="display:block;position:relative;"><div style="display:block;width:100%;padding-top:12.5%;"></div><div class="rpad rounded border" data-unit="8x1" style="position:absolute;left:0;top:0;right:0;bottom:0;overflow:hidden;"></div></div><a class="small text-muted" href="/account/join/" rel="nofollow"><i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-info-circle"> </i> Remove ads</a></div></section><section class="section3" id="database-technologies"><h3>Database Technologies<a class="headerlink" href="#database-technologies" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>If you\xe2\x80\x99re going to be moving data around, then you\xe2\x80\x99re going to be using databases a lot. Very broadly, you can separate database technologies into two categories: SQL and NoSQL.</p>\n<p>SQL databases are <strong>relational database management systems</strong> (RDBMS) that model relationships and are interacted with by using Structured Query Language, or SQL. These are commonly used to model data that is defined by relationships, such as customer order data.</p>\n<div class="alert alert-primary" role="alert">\n<p><strong>Note:</strong> If you\xe2\x80\x99d like to learn more about SQL and how to interact with SQL databases in Python, then check out the <a href="https://realpython.com/python-sql-libraries/">Introduction to Python SQL Libraries</a>.</p>\n</div>\n<p>NoSQL typically means \xe2\x80\x9ceverything else.\xe2\x80\x9d These are databases that usually store nonrelational data, such as the following:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Key-value stores like <a href="https://realpython.com/python-redis/">Redis</a> or AWS\xe2\x80\x99s <a href="https://aws.amazon.com/dynamodb/">DynamoDB</a></li>\n<li>Document stores like <a href="https://realpython.com/introduction-to-mongodb-and-python/">MongoDB</a> or <a href="https://www.elastic.co/">Elasticsearch</a></li>\n<li>Graph databases like <a href="https://neo4j.com/">Neo4j</a></li>\n<li>Other, less common data stores</li>\n</ul>\n<p>While you won\xe2\x80\x99t be required to know the ins and outs of all database technologies, you should understand the pros and cons of these different systems and be able to learn one or two of them quickly.</p>\n<p>The systems that data engineers work on are increasingly located on the cloud, and data pipelines are usually distributed across multiple servers or clusters, whether on a private cloud or not. Because of this, a prospective data engineer should understand distributed systems and cloud engineering.</p>\n</section><section class="section3" id="distributed-systems-and-cloud-engineering"><h3>Distributed Systems and Cloud Engineering<a class="headerlink" href="#distributed-systems-and-cloud-engineering" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>One of the major advantages of data engineering techniques such as ETL pipelines is that they lend themselves to the implementation of <strong>distributed systems</strong>. A common pattern is to have independent segments of a pipeline running on separate servers orchestrated by a message queue like <a href="https://www.rabbitmq.com/">RabbitMQ</a> or <a href="https://kafka.apache.org/intro">Apache Kafka</a>.</p>\n<p>It\xe2\x80\x99s essential to understand how to design these systems, what their benefits and risks are, and when you should use them.</p>\n<p>These systems require many servers, and geographically distributed teams often need access to the data they contain. Private cloud providers such as Amazon Web Services, Google Cloud, and Microsoft Azure are extremely popular tools for building and deploying distributed systems.</p>\n<p>A basic understanding of the major offerings of cloud providers as well as some of the more popular distributed messaging tools will help you find your first data engineering job. You can expect to learn these tools more in depth on the job.</p>\n<p>By now, you\xe2\x80\x99ve learned a lot about what data engineering is. But because there\xe2\x80\x99s no standard definition of the discipline, and because there are a lot of related disciplines, you should also have an idea of what data engineering is <em>not</em>.</p>\n</section></section><section class="section2" id="what-isnt-data-engineering"><h2>What Isn\xe2\x80\x99t Data Engineering?<a class="headerlink" href="#what-isnt-data-engineering" title="Permanent link"></a></h2>\n<p>Many fields are closely aligned with data engineering, and your customers will often be members of these fields. It\xe2\x80\x99s important to know your customers, so you should get to know these fields and what separates them from data engineering.</p>\n<p>Here are some of the fields that are closely related to data engineering:</p>\n<ul>\n<li>Data science</li>\n<li>Business intelligence</li>\n<li>Machine learning engineering</li>\n</ul>\n<p>In this section, you\xe2\x80\x99ll take a closer look at these fields, starting with data science.</p>\n<section class="section3" id="data-science"><h3>Data Science<a class="headerlink" href="#data-science" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>If data engineering is governed by how you move and organize huge volumes of data, then data science is governed by what you do with that data.</p>\n<p>Data scientists commonly query, explore, and try to derive insights from datasets. They may write one-off scripts to use with a specific dataset, while data engineers tend to create reusable programs using software engineering best practices.</p>\n<p>Data scientists use statistical tools such as <a href="https://realpython.com/k-means-clustering-python/">k-means clustering</a> and <a href="https://realpython.com/logistic-regression-python/">regressions</a> along with machine learning techniques. They often work with R or Python and try to derive insights and predictions from data that will guide decision-making at all levels of a business.</p>\n<div class="alert alert-primary" role="alert">\n<p><strong>Note:</strong> Do you want to explore data science? Take a look at any of the following learning paths: </p>\n<ul>\n<li><a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/data-science-python-core-skills/">Data Science With Python Core Skills</a></li>\n<li><a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/data-collection-storage/">Data Collection &amp; Storage</a></li>\n<li><a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/math-data-science/">Math for Data Science</a></li>\n<li><a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/pandas-data-science/">Pandas for Data Science</a></li>\n</ul>\n</div>\n<p>Data scientists often come from a scientific or statistical background, and their work style reflects that. They work on a project that answers a specific research question, while a data engineering team focuses on building extensible, reusable, and fast internal products.</p>\n<p>A great example of data scientists answering research questions can be found in biotech and health-tech companies, where data scientists explore data on drug interactions, side effects, disease outcomes, and more.</p>\n<div><div class="rounded border border-light" style="display:block;position:relative;"><div style="display:block;width:100%;padding-top:12.5%;"></div><div class="rpad rounded border" data-unit="8x1" style="position:absolute;left:0;top:0;right:0;bottom:0;overflow:hidden;"></div></div><a class="small text-muted" href="/account/join/" rel="nofollow"><i aria-hidden="true" class="fa fa-info-circle"> </i> Remove ads</a></div></section><section class="section3" id="business-intelligence"><h3>Business Intelligence<a class="headerlink" href="#business-intelligence" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>Business intelligence is similar to data science, with a few important differences. Where data science is focused on forecasting and making future predictions, business intelligence is focused on providing a view of the current state of the business.</p>\n<p>Both of these groups are served by data engineering teams and may even work from the same pool of data. Business intelligence, though, is concerned with analyzing business performance and generating reports from the data. These reports then help management make decisions at the business level.</p>\n<p>Like data scientists, business intelligence teams rely on data engineers to build the tools that enable them to analyze and report on data relevant to their area of focus.</p>\n</section><section class="section3" id="machine-learning-engineering"><h3>Machine Learning Engineering<a class="headerlink" href="#machine-learning-engineering" title="Permanent link"></a></h3>\n<p>Machine learning engineers are another group you\xe2\x80\x99ll come into contact with often. You may do similar work to them, or you might even be embedded in a team of machine learning engineers.</p>\n<p>Like data engineers, machine learning engineers are more focused on building reusable software, and many have a computer science background. However, they\xe2\x80\x99re less focused on building applications and more focused on building machine learning models or designing new algorithms to be used in models. </p>\n<div class="alert alert-primary" role="alert">\n<p><strong>Note:</strong> If you\xe2\x80\x99re interested in the field of machine learning, then check out the <a href="https://realpython.com/learning-paths/machine-learning-python/">Machine Learning With Python</a> learning path. </p>\n</div>\n<p>The models that machine learning engineers build are often used by product teams in customer-facing products. The data that you provide as a data engineer will be used for training their models, making your work foundational to the capabilities of any machine learning team you work with.</p>\n<p>For example, a machine learning engineer may develop a new recommendation algorithm for your company\xe2\x80\x99s product, while a data engineer would provide the data used to train and test that algorithm.</p>\n<p>One important thing to understand is that the fields you\xe2\x80\x99ve looked at here often aren\xe2\x80\x99t clear-cut. People with a data science, BI, or machine learning background may do data engineering work at an organization, and as a data engineer, you may be called upon to assist these teams in their work.</p>\n<p>You could find yourself rearchitecting a data model one day, building a data labeling tool another, and optimizing an internal deep learning framework after that. Good data engineers are flexible, curious, and willing to try new things.</p>\n</section></section><section class="section2" id="conclusion"><h2>Conclusion<a class="headerlink" href="#conclusion" title="Permanent link"></a></h2>\n<p>That completes your introduction to the field of data engineering, one of the most in-demand disciplines for people with a background or interest in computer science and technology! </p>\n<p><strong>In this tutorial, you learned:</strong></p>\n<ul>\n<li>What <strong>data engineers</strong> do</li>\n<li>Who the <strong>customers</strong> of data engineers are</li>\n<li>What <strong>skills</strong> are common to data engineering </li>\n<li>What data engineering <strong>is not</strong></li>\n</ul>\n<p>Now you\xe2\x80\x99re at the point where you can decide if you want to go deeper and learn more about this exciting field.
